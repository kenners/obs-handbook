The maternity unit at West Suffolk Hospital undertakes around 2600 deliveries each year (correct for 2016) with a Caesarean Section rate of 21% and an epidural rate in labour of around 15%. 93% of Caesarean Sections are performed under regional blockade.

The Central Delivery Suite (CDS) provides 8 delivery rooms equipped with tipping beds, piped oxygen, Entonox and suction. There is also a room with a birthing pool (one of the 8), a room for postoperative recovery of mothers having had an uncomplicated Caesarean Section under regional blockade (Room J) and a bereavement suite within the Central Delivery area. Room E is currently used as our level 2 HDU close observation room. A resuscitation trolley with defibrillator is available on CDS. A four bed midwifery led birthing unit is situated adjacent to CDS.

There is a dedicated obstetric theatre within the main theatre complex (Theatre 2). No other cases, including gynaecology, should be anaesthetised in this theatre.

Antenatal and postnatal patients are cared for on F11, with bay C being mainly used for mothers who have recently returned from theatre.

This handbook aims to outline your duties whilst on call for obstetric anaesthesia, as well as provide a summary of some of the more important guidelines. Full guidelines are available on CDS and on the Intranet in the Pink Book.

Thromboprophylaxis

Venous Thromboembolism (VTE) is a major cause of maternal mortality in the UK. In the last maternal death enquiry by MBRRACE (2009-2012) it is the leading cause of direct deaths.

Pregnancy is a risk factor for VTE an is associated with up to 4-6 x increase in risk. Whilst most women will not require thromboprophylaxis during or after their pregnancy, risk stratification is necessary to decide which women warrant prophylaxis. The threshold is lower in the postpartum period as the risk/day is higher. The majority of those who require prophylaxis can be identified by a number of well-established risk factors shown in the tables below.

Antenatal

Be aware that there will be an increasing number of women who present for delivery who are on antenatal thromboprophylaxis. A regional anaesthetic must not be given for 12 hours following any prophylactic dose (or 24 hours after a therapeutic dose). Intrapartum nutrition and antacid regime

Introduction

Historical practice for in-hospital intrapartum nutrition was to restrict oral food and fluid. This was founded on the rationale that fasting would decrease residual gastric volume, thus reducing the risk of maternal morbidity and mortality from pulmonary aspiration in the event of general anaesthesia.

Maternal death from pulmonary aspiration is rare in the United Kingdom, with no reported cases in the 2014 MBRRACE-UK report. Contributing factors for this decline are:

  • increased use of regional anaesthesia

  • improved anaesthetic training and

  • the widespread use of antacids in labour (O’Sullivan 2009).

However, with the increase in complex deliveries, especially women with obesity, it is possible that trends may increase.

A 2013 Cochrane review found that for women considered at low risk of potentially needing general anaesthesia, there was no benefit or harm associated with restricting oral fluids or food. Furthermore, there was no statistically significant difference between groups regarding the outcomes of caesarean section rate, operative vaginal birth and five-minute Apgar score of less than seven. (Cochrane 2013).

Eating and drinking may allow an improved feeling of well-being for women and may be permitted for low-risk deliveries. Eating a low residue diet for low risk deliveries is supported by NICE (2014) and the Cochrane review (2013). However, all staff must be aware that once a woman is in established labour, or once opioid analgesia has been administered by any route (including epidural), gastric emptying is significantly reduced. Therefore precautions to minimise the risk of gastric aspiration in the context of general anaesthesia do need to be undertaken.

Interventions for ketosis during labour have not been adequately studied and there is currently no consensus information on which to base treatment of women with ketosis in labour (Cochrane 2008).

For women at increased risk of potentially needing general anaesthesia, there is no evidence from randomised trials on which to base practice regarding food or fluids in labour (Cochrane 2013).

Recommendations for eating and drinking

Low risk pregnancy

Women whose pregnancies are classified as “low risk” may be fed during labour if the following criteria are present:

  • In early labour

  • No requirement for opioid analgesia (administered via any route) - No clinical indication for operative delivery

These women may be fed low fat, low residue food/drinks such as:

  • Water

  • Tea & semi/skimmed milk

  • Toast

  • Isotonic sports drinks

  • Coffee & semi/skimmed milk

  • Plain biscuits

  • Cereal & semi/skimmed milk

NICE do not recommend offering either ranitidine or antacids routinely to “low risk” women.

High risk pregnancy

Women whose pregnancy is classified as a “high risk” pregnancy should not be fed during labour, however they may have oral clear fluids. those women who have:

  • Known operative risk factors BMI > 35 - Multiple pregnancy

  • Meconium stained liquor

  • Oxytocin augmentation

  • Pregnancy induced hypertension Diabetes - Opioid analgesia by any route

  • IUGR

  • APH

  • VBAC

  • Slow progress

These women may drink non-fizzy isotonic sports drinks and water. Isotonic sports drinks help prevent maternal ketosis during labour. No food is permitted. (Kubli 2002, O’Sullivan 2002). The aim is to keep the gastric contents low and the gastric pH high.

Ranitidine 150mg (orally) should be given every 6 hours.

Cyclizine 50mg or Metoclopramide 10mg (intramuscularly) may be given for nausea or vomiting or when Pethidine has been given.

Obstetric emergencies

Failed intubation

Failed or difficult intubation is a leading cause of anaesthesia-related maternal mortality and death. It is more often a result of hypoxia than aspiration of gastric contents.

The incidence of failed tracheal intubation in the obstetric population has not decreased within the last 20 years despite the introduction of advanced airway techniques and stands at 1 in 224 in a recent UKOSS (UK Obstetric Surveillance System) study. Failed intubation is up to eight times higher than in the non-pregnant population and is associated with the older, heavier, higher BMI parturient with a Malampatti score greater than 1. A consistent cause of morbidity and mortality is both hypoxia relating to failure to intubate and ventilate and gastric aspiration which occurred in 8% of the cases of failed intubation.

Physiology of the pregnant woman means that she is more likely to have difficulties in intubation due to oedema of the airway and she will become hypoxic quicker due to a raised basal metabolic rate. In addition, gastric aspiration can occur which can cause lung injury. All women should have antacid prophylaxis before entering theatre.

All anaesthetists should have knowledge and training in the management of the failed intubation and in the use of airway aids such as the LMA, Proseal LMA, intubation LMA, video laryngoscopes and the fibreoptic laryngoscope. It is important to recognise the possibility of the difficult airway before induction of anaesthesia and call for senior help early. There was a 2.5 fold increase in failed intubation in the trainee group of anaesthetists versus the consultant group.

Preoperative management

  • Regional anaesthesia is encouraged as far as possible for operative deliveries.

  • There must be careful assessment of the airway.

  • If there is a possibility of difficult intubation, consider calling for senior help before induction of anaesthesia so that a second pair of experienced hands is available if difficulties occur.

Management of failed intubation

Maternal safety is paramount. The main aim is to prevent maternal hypoxia.

The failed intubation algorithm is displayed in the obstetric theatre and is included in the appendix and can be read in conjunction with the Difficult Airway Society Algorithm for failed intubation (DAS 2015).

The primary duty of care of the anaesthetist is to the woman, so an appropriate course of action would be to wake the woman and proceed with a regional technique.

Trainees should decide on the safest option in their hands and will be fully supported by the consultant anaesthetists if they wake the woman, even in the case of fetal distress.

Exceptions to waking the woman and continuing anaesthesia with a non-definitive airway would be life-saving surgery such as maternal haemorrhage and cardiac arrest.

It may be appropriate to continue with surgery in the case of severe fetal distress if the woman is anaesthetised and is ventilated safely with an LMA, Proseal LMA or intubating LMA if the anaesthetist is proficient with this technique and feels that it is in the best interest of the mother to continue with surgery.

  1. Remember to place the patient on the Oxford wedge before induction of anaesthesia, this positions the woman into the 'sniffing the morning air position’ which can significantly minimise the risk of a difficult intubation.

  2. If not already present, call for the assistance of a second anaesthetist. Ultimately, this should be a senior anaesthetist, but any anaesthetist can assist with the initial management of a difficult airway.

  3. Optimally position the head and neck with neck flexion and head extension, consider alternative laryngoscopes or use of a bougie.

  4. Cricoid pressure should be maintained, although may need adjusting.

  5. Do not administer a second dose of suxamethonium.

  6. MaintainoxygenationwithafacemaskandGuedelairwayifrequired.

  7. If the airway can be maintained using a facemask, then consider continuing with an inhalational anaesthetic (e.g. Sevoflurane), maintaining cricoid pressure throughout.

  8. Consider use of an iGel LMA, ProSeal or an intubating laryngeal mask.

  9. If ventilation continues to be impossible, perform a surgical airway and use transtracheal ventilation.

  10. If regurgitation occurs, suck out the pharynx and tilt the table so the woman is in a head down position.

  11. Consider the full lateral position.

Management of obstetric haemorrhage

This is a summary of the Guidelines: Antepartum Haemorrhage and Postpartum Haemorrhage.

Obstetric haemorrhage is a leading cause of maternal mortality. It accounts for approximately 10% of all direct maternal deaths. It is also a significant cause of morbidity. Postpartum haemorrhage (>500ml blood loss) affected 13% of all maternities in England 2011-12.The incidence of PPH is rising, and has nearly doubled from 7% in 2004/5 to 13% in 2011/12.

In 2009-2012 in the UK and Ireland there were 17 direct deaths due to haemorrhage. This gives a mortality of 0.49 per 100000 maternities, and a case fatality rate of 1 per 1200. There are other deaths in the most recent MBRRACE report associated with haemorrhage but are primarily attributed to other causes e.g. amniotic fluid embolus.

There have been many recommendations to reduce the morbidity and mortality including:

  • Anaemia should be investigated and iron supplementation considered to optimise haemoglobin before delivery.

  • Observations should be charted on a MEOWS chart

  • Concerns should be escalated to a senior doctor or midwife if a woman deteriorates, and there should be a named senior doctor in charge of ongoing care.

  • Fluid resuscitation and blood transfusion should not be delayed because of false reassurance from a single haemoglobin result; consider the whole clinical picture.

  • Young fit pregnant women compensate remarkably well therefore ongoing bleeding should be acted on without delay.

  • Use blood components to maintain coagulation

  • If pharmacological measures fail to control the haemorrhage, initiate surgical haemostasis sooner rather than later. Early recourse to hysterectomy is recommended if simpler medical and surgical interventions prove ineffective.

The speed with which obstetric haemorrhage can become life threatening emphasises the need for women known to be at high risk of haemorrhage to be delivered in a hospital. It is advisable that there is a blood bank, and appropriate laboratory facilities, including haematological advice and therapy on site.

The Confidential Enquiry recommends that every unit should have a protocol for the management of haemorrhage and this should be reviewed and rehearsed on a regular basis. All members of staff, including those in the blood bank, must know exactly what to do to ensure that large quantities of cross-matched blood can be delivered without delay.

It is essential that both the Consultant Obstetricians and Consultant Anaesthetists are involved at an early stage in planning the elective management of women considered to be at high-risk.

Delivery of the fetus

  • Stabilising the woman’s condition is paramount and her resuscitation takes priority. Fetal viability should be assessed as soon as feasible by Doppler or ultrasound.

  • If the fetus is dead, consider management of delivery according to maternal condition and gestational age.

  • In such a case artificial rupture of membranes and the use of syntocinon, if necessary, is appropriate. It is usually possible to achieve vaginal delivery.

  • If the fetus is alive consider gestational age, monitor the fetal heart and consider if immediate delivery is justified.

  • Mothers who have an antepartum haemorrhage are at an increased risk of postpartum haemorrhage.

  • Syntocinon infusion (40 units) should be routinely administered for a least 4 hours post-partum.

Postpartum haemorrhage (PPH)

Postpartum haemorrhage is defined as excessive bleeding from any part of the genital tract occurring at any time from the birth of the baby to the end of the puerperium. Excessive bleeding means any amount, however small, which adversely affects the mother. (Mayes 2004)

Excessive blood loss in the first 24 hours postpartum is defined as a primary or acute postpartum haemorrhage. This is the most common type and is a complication of approximately 6% of all deliveries including caesarean section. (Mayes 2004)

Excessive blood loss after the first 24 hours is termed secondary, puerperal, or late postpartum haemorrhage and accounts for approximately 1 – 3% of deliveries. (Mayes 2004)

Primary PPH

The traditional World Health Organisation definition of primary postpartum haemorrhage (PPH) is a blood loss greater than 500ml in the 24 hours following delivery. Most mothers in the UK can readily cope with a blood loss of this order and usually need no clinical intervention.

In line with the recommendations of the East of England Strategic Clinical Network for Maternity the definition of postpartum haemorrhage use at West Suffolk Hospital is as follows:

Classification of primary PPH

  • Minor: 500 – 1,000ml

  • Major: >1,000ml

    • Moderate: 1,000 – 2,000ml

    • Severe: >2,000ml

Secondary PPH

Secondary PPH is defined as abnormal or excessive bleeding from the birth canal between 24 hours and 12 weeks postnatally.

Risk factors for postpartum haemorrhage

Risk factors for PPH

  • Asian ethnicity

  • Age >40 (Primip)

  • Grand multiparity

  • BMI >35

  • Multiple pregnancy

  • Previous PPH

  • Polyhydramnios

  • Anaemia less than 90g/L (affects myometrial contractility)

  • Antepartum haemorrhage

  • Disorders of coagulation including anticoagulants

  • Past history retained placenta, abruption or placenta praevia

  • Placenta praevia or accrete in current pregnancy

  • Pre eclampsia and pregnancy induced hypertension

These pre-existing risk factors need to be documented at the booking appointment in the woman’s hand-held notes and must be taken into account when advising about the place of delivery.

An appropriate care plan must be documented and all health care providers should be aware. When risks become apparent during labour, the care plan should be modified accordingly.

Where a woman has declared her intention to decline blood or blood products, the consultant obstetrician and consultant anaesthetist should be involved antenatally and an individual management plan developed which is then documented in the maternity records.

Management

The principles of the management of postpartum haemorrhage are the same regardless of the aetiology and these are:

  1. Assessment of the woman’s condition

  2. Resuscitation of the woman

  3. Replacement of fluid

  4. Aim to stop the bleeding

  5. Ongoing care

  6. Debriefing of events

Although the information below has been grouped into appropriate headings, in reality a number of the activities undertaken will occur simultaneously. Therefore if possible have another member of staff present who will specifically record events and times.

Call for help

  • Call for help as soon as massive blood loss is recognised.

  • A midwife should summon help, if necessary by using the emergency buzzer, to gain assistance during a postpartum haemorrhage. The shift co-ordinator needs to be informed so she is able to assist.

  • If blood loss >1500ml and ongoing, use the emergency bleep by calling 2222 and calling an obstetric emergency.

  • The Obstetric Registrar should inform the Consultant Obstetrician if the woman is cardiovascularly compromised as demonstrated by hypotension, tachycardia or has a blood loss greater than 1000ml.

  • The Obstetric Registrar, Consultant Obstetrician and / or Anaesthetist may contact a Consultant Haematologist if appropriate.

Resuscitation

  • Ensure the woman is lying down, has a clear airway and is breathing.

  • Administer high flow oxygen via a mask or reservoir bag at 15 litres per minute.

  • Gain two intravenous access (minimum 16 gauge cannulae), sending blood samples as outlined below and commence intravenous fluids.

  • Venous blood is obtained as sent to haematology for estimations of:

    • Grouping and cross match (blue bottle)

    • Haemoglobin (pink bottle)

    • Clotting studies (green bottle)

  • Monitor heart rate, blood pressure and pulse oximetry as indicated below. Airway, breathing and high concentration O2 to the mother

  • Minimise aortocaval compression if still pregnant

Aim to stop the bleeding

  • Aim to stop the bleeding. Initial steps should include identification of the likely source of bleeding based on risk factors and careful evaluation of the woman’s clinical condition.

  • Initially assume uterine atony as this is by far the most common cause of excessive bleeding and is suggested by the presence of a boggy, soft uterus.

  • Give Tranexamic Acid 1 gram IV slowly and repeat if necessary according to guidelines.

Excessive bleeding due to tone

  1. Massage the uterus to stimulate a uterine contraction.

  2. A second dose of Syntometrine 1ml or Syntocinon 10 units /ml intramuscular.

  3. Empty the bladder via catheterisation.

  4. Perform bimanual compression if bleeding persists.

  5. Ergometrine 0.5mg intravenous can be used.

  6. Add 40 units of syntocinon to 36mls of Normal Saline making a volume of 40mls and infuse at a rate of 10mls per hour over 4 hours, via a Grazeby pump.

  7. (Alternatively if a Graseby pump is not available 40 units of syntocinon can be added to 500mls of Compound Sodium Lactate. Infusion via a volumetric pump at a rate of 125mls over 4 hours.)

Excessive bleeding due to trauma

  1. The woman will be placed in lithotomy position and examined by the obstetrician for lower genital tract for trauma.

  2. If the woman is still bleeding arrange a transfer to theatre for examination under anaesthesia. Where possible discuss management of care with the mother and her partner, including the possibility of the need to perform a hysterectomy.

Excessive bleeding due to retained tissue

  1. Ascertain if there were any problems in delivering the placenta and whether the placenta is complete.

  2. Arrange to transfer the woman to theatre for examination under anaesthesia and possible evacuation of retained products.

Excessive bleeding due to thrombin/coagulopathies

  1. Clotting studies are obtained to ascertain the severity of coagulopathy. See heading below “haematological considerations and target levels of treatment.” for further information with regard to haematology management.

Replacement of fluid
There is no blood fridge in theatre or CDS. Blood must be collected from blood bank.

  • Estimate blood loss by weighing pads, clots, swabs as far as possible.

  • If blood loss is great and 6 units of red cells are being requested urgently then the blood transfusion staff will ask if the massive transfusion policy should be triggered.

  • To trigger the massive transfusion policy, notify blood bank on extension #6444 or via Bleep 526 out of hours using the trigger phrase "I want to trigger the massive transfusion policy in (state location)". Give the patient’s name, hospital number and clinical details. When there is massive blood loss, the issue of products should not be delayed by tests and should be guided by the clinical picture.

Massive transfusion packs

  • First major haemorrhage pack (MHP1) consists of

    • 4 units red cells

    • 4 units of FFP

  • Major haemorrhage pack 2 consists of

    • 4 units red cells

    • 4 units FFP

    • 2 units cryoprecipitate

    • 1 pool of platelets

  • Communication between theatres and blood bank should occur frequently so that appropriate products are ordered.

  • Ideally aim to replace blood loss with packed cells.

  • Up to 2 units of Gelofusine, can be used to maintain the blood pressure until the blood is available, but if still bleeding consider O-negative blood in women who do not have Anti-c antibodies (available from Blood Bank).

  • Group specific blood can be available within 10 minutes if the woman has no antibodies.

  • Consider the use of cell salvage (see later).

  • Only Kell negative blood should be used for transfusion in women of childbearing age, owing to the high risk of alloimmunisation and subsequent haemolytic disease of the newborn (unless a woman is known to be Kell positive) (RCOG 2007)

  • Ensure when blood is obtained it is administered immediately. Blood should be used within 2 hour of removal from the laboratory fridge if it is in a chilled transportation pack or else it has to be discarded, unless returned to the laboratory prior to this time. (WSH 2011)

  • Use a blood giving set that is specifically for blood transfusions and do not use electronic infusion pumps as they may damage blood cells.

  • Give blood through blood warming equipment by using a pressure infusion with a compressor cuff. Cold blood infused faster than 100ml / minute has been reported to cause cardiac arrest. Also transfusion of cold blood will predispose to a coagulopathy.

  • Keep the woman warm, if necessary use a warming blanket.

  • Give FFP when available in the MHP1 and exclude coagulopathy by clotting studies. Do not wait for studies before giving FFP in the massive haemorrhage situation.

  • Reassess clotting studies as clinically indicated.

  • Consider Recombinant Factor VIIa in discussion with the Consultant Haematologist (see later).

  • Use a new blood giving set for the administration of platelets, fresh frozen plasma and cryoprecipitate.

  • Change the giving set if other infusions are given following a blood transfusion.

If the woman declines to have a blood transfusion / products refer to guideline “Women Who Refuse Blood Transfusion, August 2007” for further guidance.

  2. Continuous pulse oximetry monitoring recorded at least every 15 minutes

  3. Respirations every 15 minutes. At the same time be aware of the woman’s levels of consciousness.

  4. Hourly urine output

  1. Persistent pain, in spite of having had appropriate analgesia, should be regarded with suspicion, as it could be indicative of internal bleeding.

  3. Clinicians should ensure the fundal height is not rising, as this can indicate internal bleeding. At the same time the amount of lochia is assessed.

  4. Regular estimations of haemoglobin, urea and electrolytes and clotting studies are performed according to the woman’s clinical condition.

Surgical options for controlling PPH

When conservative measures have failed to control postpartum haemorrhage, definitive treatment will be surgical. These may include:

  1. Insertion of Bakri Tamponade Balloon (uterine balloon)to apply pressure and arrest bleeding. A syntocinon infusion should be continued to augment uterine contractions.

  2. Insertion of a B-Lynch suture. (B-Lynch et al 1997)

  3. Uterine artery ligation. The procedure is effective,safe and technically easier than ligation of the internal iliac artery. There are few associated complications.

  4. Internal iliac artery ligation. Postpartum success rates for this technique are poor at about 42%. Significant operative morbidity is associated with the technique and includes nerve injury, gluteal necrosis, inadvertent ligation of common iliac artery, cardiac arrest and prolonged blood loss and operative time.

  5. Hysterectomy

Further medical options for controlling PPH

  1. Ensure coagulopathy has been investigated and vigorously treated with fresh frozen plasma, platelets or cryoprecipitate in consultation with haematologists

  2. Ensure intravenous fluids are warm

  3. Ensure the woman is kept warm as acidosis and hypothermia will adversely affect blood clotting

  4. If there has been a massive transfusion consider Vitamin K 10mg intravenously

  5. Give Tranexamic acid 1 gram intravenously slowly acutely and then three times a day as required

  6. Consider Recombitant VII (Eptacogalfa if coagulation studies are normal and in the face of continued haemorrhage, after discussion with Consultant Haematologist.

  7. Cell salvage is recommended for women in whom an intraoperative blood loss of more than 1000ml is anticipated

  8. Cell salvage should only be used by healthcare teams who use it regularly and have the necessary expertise and experience. Consent should be obtained and an information leaflet given. Its use in obstetrics should be subject to audit and monitoring. (RCOG 2007)

  9. Intervention by radiological management by uterine artery embolisation are not presently practiced within West Suffolk Hospital

Anaesthetic management

  • Regional anaesthesia should only be considered if the mother is fully resuscitated and cardiovascularly stable. In virtually all cases where there has been massive obstetric haemorrhage, a general anaesthetic is the technique of choice.

  • A Consultant Anaesthetist must be informed prior to the transfer of a woman to theatre.

  • A medical, surgical and obstetric history is obtained from the mother and the anaesthetic technique to be used is explained.

  • Ensure the following has been carried out:

    • The woman is cannulated with two large 16 gauge cannulae

    • Blood is available

    • Consider cell salvage

Total and high spinal

High blocks following spinal or epidural anaesthesia and total spinals are potentially life threatening and require immediate treatment. Women may experience difficulty with their breathing and become significantly hypotensive. If maternal hypoxia can be prevented and normal blood pressure restored quickly, the baby may later be delivered without any subsequent problems.

This can occur when:

  • An epidural has been performed and there has been an unrecognised dural puncture by the Tuohy needle or epidural catheter and full epidural doses of local anaesthetic have been deposited in the subarachnoid space.

  • Spinal anaesthesia has been administered following an epidural block. There is no consensus of opinion as to the dosage of spinal anaesthesia that should be administered following an inadequate epidural. Therefore inadvertently a high spinal could occur.

  • There is an unexpected high block with spinal or epidural anaesthesia at caesarean section.

Prevention of total or high spinal anaesthesia

Epidural analgesia boluses should be given slowly and in incremental doses. If higher concentrations of local anaesthetic (0.25% chirocaine or higher), are used for epidural analgesia, give an appropriate volume test dose first.

For spinal anaesthesia:

  • Use hyperbaric local anaesthetic

  • Avoid excessive barbotage

  • Maintain/exaggerate thoracic kyphosis

  • Do not inject dose during a contraction, maternal coughing or maternal Valsalva manoeuvre as this can increase cephalad spread of local anaesthetic.

  • Care should be taken when giving a spinal anaesthetic after a failed epidural top up. Dosage and volume should be decreased accordingly and should usually be in the 1 to 1.5ml range. When the spinal is inserted in the lateral position, consider placing the table in the reverse trendelenburg position (head upwards) or use the Oxford wedge to decrease the risk of cephalad spread of hyperbaric local anaesthetic.

Identification of total or high spinal anaesthesia

The woman can:

  • become anxious and complain of difficulty in breathing

  • complain of numbness, tingling or weakness in her arms

  • unable to maintain an oxygen saturation greater than 95% on 100% oxygen

  • can only whisper but no longer phonate (pronounce words)

  • reduced level of consciousness

  • hypotension is common and may be profound

  • bradycardia can occur

Management of total or high spinal anaesthesia

If the woman develops extensive motor and sensory block, profound hypotension, apnoea and loss of consciousness – act quickly.
Do not tilt the woman head downward.

  • If a total spinal anaesthesia occurs on the labour ward, emergency bleep the anaesthetist immediately on Bleep 770

  • Urgently contact the ODP covering obstetrics via Bleep 840, explain what critical incident has occurred

  • Contact the Obstetric Registrar on Bleep 670

  • Send for:

    • The resuscitation trolley

    • Emergency anaesthetic drugs which are found in the CDS drugs fridge and drugs cupboard

    • Emergency intubation equipment from Room J

    • The ODP may bring the anaesthetic grab bag and emergency drugs from theatres as a back up to equipment available above

  • Reassure the woman as awareness can still be an issue.

  • Place the woman in a supine position with left uterine displacement.

  • Assess and treat the woman following Airway, Breathing, Circulation (ABC) principles.

  • Give oxygen by bag and mask.

  • Not all women with a high spinal need immediate induction of anaesthesia, if the woman is conscious, breathing well, cardiovascularly stable and managing well with the situation then consider observing closely, symptoms may resolve before the need to intubate and ventilate and a vaginal delivery may still be possible to manage.

  • If it is likely that induction of general anaesthesia will be necessary:

    • Ask the birthing partner to leave the room, preferably with a midwife or HCA to accompany them.

    • Apply cricoid pressure and prepare for intubation.

    • Have suction available.

    • Give lower than routine doses of Thiopentone, plus Suxamethonium 1mg/kg and intubate with a rapid sequence induction.

  • Intravenous fluids and pressor agents, such as Ephedrine or Phenylephrine, are administered to restore an adequate blood pressure.

  • Continue to anaesthetise the woman until she regains sufficient motor control to maintain respiration.

  • Continuous cardiotocography (CTG) monitoring if appropriate.

  • Consider urgent delivery of the baby.

  • The woman should be transferred to the Intensive Care Unit (ICU) until fully recovered.

Postnatal management

Once a mother has fully recovered she is transferred to the postnatal ward. No extra postpartum precautions are needed and routine postnatal care is given. A full explanation of events should be discussed with the woman by a Consultant.

  • Consider if there is severe abdominal pain after delivery, if there is shock out of proportion to the apparent blood loss or unexplained bradycardia.

  • Blood loss is often underestimated.

  • Give oxygen and establish venous access.

  • Elevate presenting part of fetus away from cord by obstetrician/midwife

Uterine rupture

  • Consider if there is sudden cessation of uterine activity or abdominal pain between contractions, sometimes despite the epidural.

  • Give oxygen, establish venous access and send blood for cross matching of 4-6 units blood.

  • Send for senior help.

  • Diagnose and treat shock. The mother’s welfare is paramount and shock should be treated so as to render induction of anaesthesia safe.

  • In extremely rare circumstances, operative resuscitation may be required.

  • Prepare for immediate induction of general anaesthesia, for caesarean section and repair.

  • Expect massive obstetric haemorrhage and manage accordingly.

  • This is rare and thought to be an anaphylactoid reaction triggered by maternal exposure to fetal antigens.

    • Severe hypoxaemia

    • Disseminated intravascular coagulation

Labour analgesia

Regional analgesia in labour

Introduction

The regional techniques employed at the West Suffolk Hospital are intended to preserve motor function whilst alleviating the pain of all stages of labour. The anaesthetic department provide a 24 hour obstetric epidural service and aim to provide epidural analgesia within 30 minutes of being notified.

The Labour Suite provides programmed intermittent epidural boluses supported by patient controlled epidural analgesia (PIEB and PCEA). PIEB has been shown to decrease breakthrough pain requiring physician top-up, reduce local anaesthetic consumption without compromising analgesic efficacy and increase patient satisfaction by offering autonomy in pain relief during labour.

We use a pre-prepared low dose epidural infusion with Levobupivacaine (0.1%) and Fentanyl (2 micrograms/ml).

On occasions, the low doses of Levobupivacaine and Fentanyl can prove insufficient, so higher concentrations of Levobupivacaine (0.25%) and or a bolus of Fentanyl (50 – 100 micrograms) may be indicated on Labour Suite.

You must have training in the use of the epidural pump and have the required competencies signed off. Please see the lead Obstetric Consultant Anaesthetist as soon as possible after your arrival.

Indications for regional anasthesia

  • Maternal request for pain relief

  • Twin and breech pregnancies

  • Hypertensive disorders

  • Vaginal birth after Caesarean Section (VBAC)

  • Augmentation of labour

  • Obesity (Consider if BMI is 35 and above / anticipation of caesarean section)

Relative contraindications to regional anaesthesia

  • Thrombocytopenia

    • Platelet count 80 – 100 x 109/L - need a recent normal clotting screen before an epidural is performed.

    • In a woman with a rapidly falling platelet count, the platelet count and coagulation screening must be carried out within 4 hours prior to the epidural insertion.

    • Platelets < 80 x 109/L discussion with the on call consultant anaesthetist is needed to decide on the appropriate course of action.

  • Thromboprophylaxis

    • Unfractionated heparin - no block within 4 hours

    • Low molecular weight heparin - no block within 12 hours

    • Low dose aspirin (75mg) daily alone is not a contraindication to regional analgesia/anaesthesia

With the new national VTE guidelines, many more women will be taking antenatal LMWH. Please specifically ask the question in your assessment.

  • Therapeutic dose low molecular weight heparin

    • This may be given once or twice daily. Regional blockade should not be undertaken until 24 hours have elapsed since the last dose.

  • Hypovolaemia

    • Epidural anaesthesia may only be considered once the intravascular volume is restored and maintained.

  • Generalised septicaemia

    • Discuss with consultant anaesthetist

  • Valvular heart disease

    • A Consultant Anaesthetist must be informed before regional anaesthesia is employed.

  • Supervision by staff

    • A woman must be given one to one care by a midwife trained in epidural management.

    • An epidural should not be undertaken if one to one care is not available.

    • If a woman is refused an epidural anaesthetic the Consultant Anaesthetist responsible for obstetric anaesthesia is to be informed and a Trust Risk Reporting form completed on Datix.

Contraindications to regional anaesthesia

Absolute

  • Maternal refusal

  • Infection at the site of insertion

  • Full anticoagulation (therapeutic Tinzaparin or Enoxaparin within 24 hrs) * Tinzaparin thromboprophylaxis within 12 hours

Controversial

  • Back pain and previous back surgery

    • There is no evidence that epidural anaesthesia causes back pain, or exacerbates existing back pain. Women with previous back injury or surgery should be warned there may be an increased risk of patchy block or dural tap.

    • If metal work is in place in the back this may be a relative contraindication because of both poor spread of local anaesthetic and the risk of infection.

  • Spina bifida

    • Epidural analgesia should not be denied to these women.

    • The extent and type of lesion should be defined and any pre existing neurological deficit should be accurately documented.

    • Epidurals can be sited above lumbar anomalies but there should be an awareness of unpredictable spread of local anaesthesia. It may cause patchy block or incomplete sacral analgesia.

    • All cases should be referred to the Consultant Anaesthetist on call.

  • Any mother considered likely to have problems with regional anaesthesia or have medical problems should be referred to the obstetric anaesthetic clinic antenatally.

Programmed intermittent epidural bolus (PIEB) analgesia

Before starting PIEB analgesia, pain relief in the labouring woman can be achieved by either loading the epidural or by a combined spinal and epidural technique.

Standard epidural (epidural loading dose)

The routine method for initiating epidural analgesia is by the epidural loading dose.

  • The epidural is sited using an aseptic technique with the mother in the sitting or lateral position.

  • 0.5% chlorhexidine should be used for skin sterilisation. This should be poured into the tray or separate gallipot pot away from your other equipment, used and discarded before any other solutions or equipment is used minimise the risk of arachnoiditis.

  • The loss of resistance technique is a matter of individual choice for the anaesthetist; however, the preferred method for trainee anaesthetists is loss of resistance to saline.

  • Aim to leave 3 – 4 cms epidural catheter in the epidural space.

  • The initial epidural loading dose is 10ml-20ml of the 0.1% Levobupivacaine with 2mcg/ml Fentanyl solution (premixed solution in 250ml bag).

  • This initial epidural loading dose can be administered using the clinician bolus facility on the epidural pump or by withdrawing bolus amount from the epidural infusion bag.

  • Strict asepsis must be observed to avoid contamination of the epidural infusion bag.

  • All subsequent boluses must be administered through the epidural pump as epidural infusion bags do not contain preservative and are not suitable for multi-dosing.

Combined spinal epidural analgesia (CSE)

Indications for the CSE technique may include: * Severe maternal distress * Late stage of labour including second stage * Previous failed attempt at siting epidural needle or catheter - Ambulatory epidural analgesia.

Either a ‘two-space technique’ or ‘needle through needle technique’ can be used. Unless trainee anaesthetists have had training and been supervised in the ‘needle through needle’ technique, the two-space technique is preferred.

At an interspinous space below L3, an intrathecal injection of 1ml of 0.25% Levobupivacaine with 15mcg Fentanyl is given using a fine gauge pencil-point spinal needle. Use of a 26G or 27G needle is preferred as the risk of post-dural puncture headache may be less than for a 25G needle.

After the intrathecal injection, the epidural is sited in the standard way described above in a suitable lumbar interspace. It is advisable to flush the epidural catheter with about 2mls of 0.9% saline to ensure that blood clots do not block the catheter.

When using a CSE technique, you must either return to commence the epidural once the discomfort has begun to recur or start the pump, removing the handset so that the first PIEB is given after an hour.

Maintenance of epidural analgesia

Following the initial loading dose, initiate PIEB with PCEA to maintain analgesia throughout labour. The correct and safe use of this mode of analgesia will require cooperation from the woman.

Patient controlled epidural analgesia should only be instituted when satisfactory epidural analgesia has been established and maternal observations (MEWOS) and the fetal heart rate are stable.

Satisfactory maternal observations include absence of symptomatic maternal hypotension, ability to straight leg raise both legs and a height of sensory block to cold that is at T10 or below.

Post dural puncture headache

If the dura mater is inadvertently punctured during insertion of an epidural this is known as a dural puncture. If the dural puncture is made by the Tuohy needle, this is usually very apparent when the cerebrospinal fluid escapes. It may be less obvious if the dura is accidentally punctured by the epidural catheter.

During a spinal anaesthesia the dura is intentionally breached. In this situation post dural puncture headache is less likely due to the use of finer needles. However, even though much finer needles are used, some women may still experience headaches. It is thought that this is due to the reduction in pressure of the cerebrospinal fluid.

The headaches may be severe and distressing and need to be managed appropriately.

The incidence of accidental dural puncture is around 1.5% with the post dural puncture headache being estimated as approximately half this rate. However the actual incidence is hard to detect, even when studied prospectively.

Every woman who has regional anaesthesia should be reviewed by an anaesthetist in order to ascertain complications, including post dural puncture headache.

Features of a post dural puncture headache

  • There is a distinct association with increased pain when the woman sits and stands, usually within 24 hours.

  • Pain may be frontal or circumferential. There may also be neck pain.

  • Nausea and vomiting may occur.

  • There may be a degree of photophobia.

  • Cranial nerve palsies – VI nerve is most commonly affected, but tinnitus and deafness have also been described.

  • The woman feels unwell and is unable to care for her baby.

Postpartum management of headache

If a woman has a headache post delivery a consultant anaesthetist should be informed and these women reviewed daily by an anaesthetist until the headache has resolved. Other causes of headache should be excluded, such as:

Differential diagnosis of postpartum headache

  • Non-specific postnatal headache.

  • Migraine (history of migraine, unilateral pulsatile headache, associated with vasomotor signs).

  • Pre-eclampsia (recent labour complicated with condition up to 10 days postpartum).

  • Septic and aseptic meningitis (increasing headache, nausea, vomiting and neck stiffness).

  • Intracranial haemorrhage/mass lesion (signs of intracranial hypertension).

  • Cerebral vein thrombosis (a headache of increasing intensity, convulsions, intracranial hypertension, deteriorating consciousness and fever. MRI and MRA are diagnostic).

  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage

  • Meningitis

  • Cortical vein thrombosis

  • Cerebral tumour

  • Subdural haematoma

  • Postnatal depression headache.

  • Pneumoencephalus (sudden headache, due to injection of air in the subdural or subarachnoid space, associated with epidural using loss of resistance to air technique. Headache is worse in sitting position and relieved by lying down. It disappears after few hours).

Obtain an accurate history from the woman. A full examination is carried out including blood pressure, temperature, urinalysis and neurological examination.

If the diagnosis of post dural puncture headache is made, encourage oral fluids such as tea, coffee and coca cola, as the caffeine in these drinks can help with the symptoms. Intravenous fluids maybe indicated if oral fluids cannot be tolerated.

The woman should lie flat for 24 hours, after which she is reviewed again by the anaesthetist. If the headache persists an epidural blood patch might be considered. Prescribe regular oral analgesics such as Paracetamol, Codeine phosphate, Diclofenac or Ibuprofen.

Prescribe a regular laxative to avoid straining during bowel action.

Epidural blood patch

This is regarded as the most definitive treatment and must be performed in theatre by the most senior available anaesthetist available, but is advisable that this should not be the anaesthetist causing the original dural tap.

Ensure the woman is apyrexial and there are no signs of systemic infection.

Gain consent and inform women of the following:

  • There is always the risk of a further dural puncture. (1:100)

  • They may feel a sensation of fullness in the back on injection of the blood.

  • Occasionally they may experience tinnitus.

  • Quoted rates of success range from 48 – 70%, with 30% of women requiring a second patch.

  • No woman should receive more than three blood patches.

The procedure requires two operators and a fully aseptic technique by both.

Women should be asked to adopt the lateral position – it is cruel to sit them up with a postural headache.

The level of injection should be at the site of the previous epidural or at one space lower. 20mls of blood is withdrawn from an arm vein by the second operator (using a meticulous aseptic technique) after the epidural space has been confirmed by the first. The blood is slowly injected into the epidural space. Stop injecting if the woman feels significant discomfort. A further attempt at injection can be made if the discomfort subsides.

The woman should lie flat for 2 hours post blood patch, then slowly mobilise. The woman is reviewed daily by an anaesthetist until discharged home.

She should be warned against heavy lifting or housework for one week. Laxatives may be helpful to avoid straining during bowel action.

If any problems recur once discharged home, the midwives should contact the on call anaesthetist.

Remifentanil analgesia in labour

Remifentanil is an opioid with ultrafast onset, has organ independent metabolism and short elimination time, which gives it an ideal profile for use as an intravenous opioid in labour. It crosses the placenta, but does not accumulate in the neonate. Only minimal or no side effects have been recorded in the baby. In a survey of Lead Obstetric Anaesthetists undertaken on behalf of the Obstetric Anaesthetists' Association in 2010, it was demonstrated that 36% of respondents offered Remifentanil Patient Controlled Analgesia (PCA) in labour and that 48% of the remaining units intended to introduce it in the future. However, Remifentanil remains unlicensed in obstetrics.

Within the West Suffolk Hospital Trust, a Remifentanil PCA is used for labour analgesia only. There will be very small numbers of women in whom conventional labour analgesia is contraindicated. A Remifentanil PCA provides them with a practical effective alternative. In some studies it has been shown to provide superior analgesia to Pethidine. However, as with administration of all opioids, especially via the intravenous route, safe delivery of the drug and careful monitoring of the patient is essential to avoid complications.

Indications

  • Analgesia for labour when as epidural is contraindicated, for example in women with coagulopathies, certain spinal abnormalities, some forms of spinal surgery, sepsis sufficient to preclude the use of an epidural.

  • Analgesia in labour when current accepted methods have failed and further analgesia is deemed essential by attending professionals.

  • This technique is not offered to those women, who by personal choice, do not want an epidural.

Contraindications

  • Allergy to Remifentanil (extremely rare).

  • Patient unable or unwilling to use the PCA handset in the appropriate manner.

  • Reduced level of consciousness e.g. intracranial pathology.

  • Long acting opioids during the preceding 4 hours. However, if a patient is in extreme pain within 4 hours of a long acting opioid, then the use of a Remifentanil PCA must be discussed with a Consultant Anaesthetist, prior to its use.

  • Inability to provide 1:1 trained midwifery care.

  • Women delivering in the Midwifery Led Birthing Unit

Cautions

  • Significant comorbidity e.g. cardiac or respiratory disease, which would make her more susceptible to the side effects of Remifentanil.

  • Any woman with pre-eclampsia. The obstetricians should be consulted if this is deemed to be the only appropriate method of analgesia for these women.

  • Pre-term labour less than 36 weeks, unless the fetus is non-viable - obstetricians should be consulted.

  • Intrauterine growth retardation - obstetricians should be consulted.

If there are any concern the woman should be discussed with the Consultant Anaesthetist on-call before commencing PCA.

Requirements

  • The woman must be in a clinical delivery room on Labour Suite with fixed oxygen and suction outlets available with continuous 1:1 trained midwifery care.

  • A paediatrician should be called for the delivery if the Remifentanil PCA has been used during second stage of labour. The anaesthetist should record use of a Remifentanil PCA in the Obstetric Anaesthetic Register, so women can be followed up.

  • The patient must be issued with, and have read the patient information sheet once the need for the Remifentanil PCA becomes evident and has been agreed by the anaesthetist and midwife.

    • Inform the patient that although the drug is unlicensed in pregnancy, there is wide and growing experience of using the drug safely for labour analgesia.

    • She should be informed of the side effects of dizziness and drowsiness. That pain relief is unlikely to be total, but it has been found to be better than the pain relief accorded by Pethidine.

  • She will be given supplemental oxygen continuously when a PCA is in use.

The demand button must be pushed on first suspicion of a contraction and not wait until the contraction peaks (similar to Entonox timings). Instruction that no birthing partner or non-anaesthetic staff should press the demand button on the handset.

Anaesthesia for caesarean sections

This guideline is a summary of: Regional Anaesthesia for Caesarean Section / General Anaesthesia for Caesarean Section / Elective Caesarean section / Emergency Caesarean Section

Introduction

An increasing number of women are now undergoing delivery by elective caesarean section, for various reasons. Caesarean section is major abdominal surgery with associated risks and therefore appropriate care and follow-up is required. The estimated fatality rate following elective caesarean section is reported as 136 deaths per 100,000 maternities, relative risk rate 2.8 (CEMACH 2004).

All women with a high anaesthetic risk must be referred to the Obstetric Anaesthetist for assessment in advance of delivery at a monthly clinic. (See guideline Referral of Obstetric Women to the Anaesthetic Department)

  • Time of testing the block and of adequate height and density, or induction of general anaesthesia

  • Time of delivery, condition of baby and gender of baby

  • Time oxytocin drug was administered

  • Time antibiotics were administered.

  • Time analgesia was administered.

Resuscitation of the newborn

The responsibility of the anaesthetist is to the woman, her safety, providing adequate and effective analgesia, and the safe conduct of the third stage of labour, with the giving of oxytocic drugs to arrest haemorrhage.

The anaesthetist may assist at resuscitation of the newborn, as long as maternal safety is assured and supervision is delegated to an appropriate assistant.

Postoperative medication

To control post operative pain, analgesia should be given at regular intervals. The following analgesia is prescribed by the anaesthetist prior to transfer to the ward area:

  • Regular prescribed analgesia:

    • Ibuprofen 400mg three times daily (unless contraindicated)

    • Paracetamol 1g intravenously after delivery of the baby, then orally four times daily. (Maximum 4 grams in 24 hours)

It is prudent to wait 6 hours before IV morphine is given. However, if a woman is in pain and simple analgesics have proved inadequate, morphine maybe given if the woman is not unduly sedated and her respiratory rate is more than 12 per minute.

    • Cyclizine 50mg intramuscular or intravenous three times daily

It is the responsibility of the obstetric team to prescribe thromboprophylaxis after a post- natal risk assessment has been performed. However, it is vital that the anaesthetist provides information about the timing of the spinal injection and timing of removal of the epidural catheter.

Regional anaesthesia for elective caesarean section

Single ‘shot’ (dose) spinals, epidurals and combined spinal-epidurals are used in this unit. NICE suggest that women having a caesarean section should be offered regional anaesthesia because it is safer and results in less maternal and neonatal morbidity than general anaesthesia.

Side effects and complications

  • Hypotension resulting in nausea and vomiting or light-headedness is common.

  • Itching, particularly if spinal diamorphine is used.

  • Shivering.

  • Failed or inadequate spinal block. This can be treated by either:

    • Topping up the epidural or epidural component of a combined spinal epidural.

    • Giving a second spinal injection with a decreased dose of local anaesthetic solution.

    • Giving the woman a general anaesthetic

    • Emphasise to the woman that surgery will not proceed until the block is effective.

    • Also if pain is experienced intra-operatively then a range of measures can be undertaken including general anaesthesia.

  • Post dural puncture headache occurs in about 1:100 (epidural) to 1:200 (spinal) cases.

  • Neurological damage is rare. The incidence of permanent damage is 1:80,000 to 1:320,000 cases.

Procedure

  • Ensure Sodium Citrate 30mls 0.3M has been given to the woman.

  • Insert a large bore intravenous cannula, ideally 16 gauge.

  • Commence Hartmann’s solution prior to regional anaesthesia.

  • Vasopressor drugs should be used prophylactically to treat hypotension as necessary.

  • Phenylephrine as an infusion is the vasopressor of choice, alternatively ephedrine can be given in 6mg doses to treat hypotension.

  • Phenylepherine infusion of 100mcg/ml. is used with the starting rate at 20mls/hr. This is then titrated according to blood pressure and patient symptoms.

  • Record and document the woman’s blood pressure as a baseline reading.

  • Perform regional anaesthesia - a spinal, a combined spinal epidural (CSE) or an epidural with the woman in the lateral or sitting position using a fully aseptic technique.

  • 0.5% Chlorhexidine solution should be used and allowed to dry prior to neuraxial blockade. Chlorhexidine solution must be kept distant to all neuraxial equipment to reduce the risk of adhesive archnoiditis.

  • When performing an epidural or a CSE, loss of resistance to saline is the preferred technique in this institution.

  • A spinal should not be performed above the L2 - L3 interspace as this can be associated with an increased risk of conus injury.

  • A suitable spinal anaesthetic is (11.5 – 12.5mg) 2.3 - 2.5mls 0.5% hyperbaric Bupivacaine plus Diamorphine 300mcg. Diamorphine can provide up to 6 hours of post- operative pain relief. Consider reducing the dose of heavy bupivacaine if the woman is of particularly short stature.

  • 5mg Diamorphine should be diluted to 5mls with 0.9% Saline in a 5ml syringe – 1mg perml concentration – 0.3ml of this solution is measured in a 1ml syringe (300mcg) then added to the 0.5% heavy Bupivacaine.

  • An alternative to diamorphine is 15 - 25mcg of Fentanyl.

  • If performing an epidural, the catheter should be inserted to a maximum depth of 3 – 4cm within the epidural space, aspirated to ensure absence of blood or cerebrospinal fluid, and then secured to the woman’s back.

  • Position the woman on the theatre table with left uterine displacement, either with a wedge, or 15 degree table tilt to avoid aorto-caval compression.

Potential problems and complications during procedure

Problem Solution

Hypotension

This may manifest as nausea or vomiting or fetal bradycardia. Treatment should include intravenous fluids and phenylephrine or ephedrine.

Persistent fetal bradycardia should be treated with maternal left lateral position and oxygen. The Obstetricians should be notified of fetal distress.

Paraesthesia or pain on insertion of epidural needle or catheter

If this is of more than a transient nature, then the epidural should be abandoned and insertion attempted in another space. This should be documented in the procedure note.

Catheter in an epidural vein

Flush the catheter clear with normal saline and withdraw a millimetre at a time. Minimum catheter length in space = 3 cms Repeat the aspiration to ensure no further blood is obtained. If blood continues to be aspirated, abandon the interspace and try another.

Inadequate block height

If there is no epidural catheter in situ various manoeuvres can be tried although none are foolproof. This has to be achieved before surgery commences.

  • Tipping the woman slightly head down, drawing the knees up or rolling the woman from side to side can help. Be aware of sudden increase in block height.

  • If the block height is still inadequate, discuss with the consultant on call a second spinal in the lateral position. If this is warranted then consider a smaller dose of local anaesthetic i.e. 1-1.5mls of 0.5% heavy Bupivacaine

  • If an epidural catheter is in place inject 5mls 0.5% Levo- bupivacaine into the epidural space separated by 5 minute intervals until the desired level is reached

Suspected total spinal

Difficulty in moving arms, low BP & breathlessness

Stop infusion. Call Obstetric Emergency and refer to Total and high spinal anaesthesia guideline. Call 770 and duty consultant anaesthetist.

Unintentional Subarachnoid Puncture

There are two approaches to this problem, either:

  • Withdraw the epidural and locate in a different space. Inform the woman and midwife of dural puncture and anticipate post dural puncture headache.

  • If the procedure has been difficult and regional analgesia is desirable, the catheter can be left in the sub arachnoid space and topped up with appropriate SPINAL doses of local anaesthetic and opiates. A consultant must be informed prior to its use by a trainee. The catheter should be clearly marked: SPINAL CATHETER FOR ANAESTHETIST TOP UP ONLY. The catheter must be removed before the woman leaves theatre. These women must be followed up regularly by a senior anaesthetist post-delivery.

Assessment of regional anaesthetic block

Sensory testing

  • Test the block after 5 minutes. Note that the spinal block may start being established within 5 minutes; however the epidural may take up to 20 minutes.

  • Testing should be with ice and a block extending bilaterally to the T4 dermatome must be achieved.

  • Either test deep pressure using a blunt drawing up needle/ neuro-tip and record the level of the block.

  • Record the block height to two modalities of sensation - pain and touch, on the anaesthetic chart.

Motor testing

  • The ability of the woman to move her legs against gravity should be assessed and graded. The Bromage score should be recorded on the anaesthetic chart.

  • To assess the level of the sacral block, ask the woman to try and perform a pelvic floor exercise.

Bilateral warm legs due to vasodilatation is a sign of good sympathetic block. The assessment of the block should be noted on the anaesthetic chart.

Intraoperative breakthrough pain

There are a number of measures, which can be employed, but the surgeon should be asked to stop if the woman is uncomfortable and only allowed to resume once she is pain free.

  • Entonox

  • Epidural top up - as above.

  • Short acting opioid

    • Alfentanil can be given after the baby is born. Dilute 1mg to 10mls with 0.9% Saline for injections and titrate 100-200 micrograms (1-2ml) boluses.

    • Fentanyl in 25 micrograms boluses can be used as an alternative.

  • Local anaesthetic infiltration towards the end of surgery, the surgeon can infiltrate the peritoneum/ skin incision with 1% Lignocaine.

  • If anxiety is thought to be a significant problem, midazolam 1mg bolus can be considered. Supplemental oxygen must also be given. The woman/ partner must be informed of the antegrade amnesic effect of this as it could cause significant psychological upset.

  • General anaesthesia. This can be performed at any point. If pain is perceived near the start of the operation, before the baby is delivered, consider general anaesthesia as a first option.

The time and site of pain should be recorded on the anaesthetic chart, plus any measures offered which were refused or accepted plus their efficacy.

If a woman has experienced any pain she should be reviewed post operatively and any problems (or not) documented in her maternity notes.

A consultant anaesthetist should also be informed if there is cause for concern.

General anaesthesia for elective caesarean sections

Although general anaesthesia for elective caesarean section is uncommon, it may be indicated by the obstetric or anaesthetic situation, or at the request of the patient.

It may be appropriate to discuss the risks and benefits of both regional and general anaesthesia, however if the woman expresses grave concern with regional techniques, and if there are no significant contraindications then her request for a general anaesthetic should be accommodated.

Preoperative

  • Ensure all anaesthetic equipment is available and in working order.

  • Ensure adequate airway aids are available, particularly a short handled laryngoscope, Polio and McCoy Blades.

  • Ensure you are familiar with the failed intubation drill.

Intraoperative

  • Position the woman on the theatre table with a 15 degree left lateral table tilt to reduce aorto-caval compression.

  • Take time to optimise the patient’s head and neck position and consider use of the Oxford HELP intubation pillow to improve the view at laryngoscopy.

  • Obtain large bore intravenous access using at least a 16 gauge cannula, and commence a crystalloid infusion.

  • Establish full monitoring as per AAGBI guidelines to include non-invasive blood pressure, ECG and pulse oximetry. Note the baseline blood pressure and heart rate.

  • The WHO checklist must be undertaken BEFORE induction of anaesthesia.

  • Catheterise before induction of anaesthesia. This will decrease the time between induction and knife to skin and hence the amount of general anaesthetic agents that accumulate in the baby. Catheterisation can be undertaken whilst pre-oxygenating the patient.

  • Antibiotic prophylaxis intravenously prior to skin incision

    • Cefuroxime 1.5 g and Metronidazole 500mg

    • If a penicillin allergy, Gentamicin 160mg and Metronidazole 500mg (if booking weightless than 50kg, 120mg Gent)

    • Add Teicoplanin if MRSA The woman may be catheterised before induction of anaesthesia.

Induction of anaesthesia

  • Pre-oxygenate for 3 minutes.

  • Induction should be in theatre, and theatre staff should be quiet and prepared for immediate commencement of surgery.

  • The obstetric team should be scrubbed and the patient draped for surgery.

  • Rapid sequence induction with Thiopentone, Suxamethonium and cricoid pressure.

  • Intubation with a 7mm cuffed endotracheal tube (ETT). Consider using a smaller tube for women with pre-eclampsia as they have the potential for airway oedema.

  • Once intubation is confirmed using capnography, and the ETT is tied and secured in place, the obstetrician can then proceed with skin incision.

  • If pre-eclamptic the intubation response will need blunting by either opiates or an alternative at both induction and emergence (see pre-eclampsia guideline). The paediatrician should be informed.

Maintenance of anaesthesia

  • Carrier gas: FiO2 0.5 before delivery of the baby, 0.3 – 0.5 after delivery.

  • N2O should generally be used instead of air.

  • Volatile: Sevoflurane 0.7 to 1.0 MAC

  • Muscle relaxant: Atracurium or Rocuronium

Treatment of hypotension

  • Intravenous ephedrine or phenylephrine should be used in the management of hypotension during caesarean section.

Prevention of PONV

  • Cyclizine may be used to prevent post-operative nausea and vomiting (PONV).

  • Ensure adequate rehydration with IV crystalloids to further reduce the risk of PONV, however execute caution in patients with pre-eclampsia.

Syntocinon

  • 5 units of Syntocinon intravenously should be given slowly once the cord has been clamped.

  • Consider commencing an infusion of 40 units Syntocinon in 40ml Normal Saline over 4 hours via a syringe pump, particularly if there is a risk of atonic uterus or a post- partum haemorrhage.

Analgesia

  • 1g of Paracetamol should be given intravenously after delivery. Ensure that 4hours have elapsed since any previous dose.

  • Morphine 10 – 15mg should be given intravenously after delivery.

  • 100mg of Diclofenac should be given per rectum by the surgical team on completion of surgery, ensure no contraindications to NSAIDs. Avoid NSAIDs in pre-eclamptic patients and those who have had major haemorrhage.

  • Transversus abdominus plane blocks may be sited prior to emergence and extubation, thereby providing analgesia and reducing postoperative opioid requirements.

Extubation

  • The woman is recovered either in a sitting position or in the left lateral position. Extubation should occur once the neuromuscular blockade is fully reversed, the patient is breathing adequately and she is fully awake and obeying commands.

  • Ensure engagement during discussion and completion of WHO checklist part 3 at the end of the procedure.

Emergency caesarean section

Appropriate and expedient timing of emergency caesarean section is necessary for good fetal and maternal outcome. If timing allows, a regional block technique is safer than general anaesthesia, as it results in less maternal and neonatal morbidity.

If a trial of vaginal delivery is undertaken in theatre, the possibility of an emergency caesarean section needs to be anticipated therefore appropriate working regional blocks should be instituted before the obstetric procedure begins.

Preoperative care

  • Explanation of events and verbal consent is obtained and recorded in the woman’s health records by the obstetric registrar.

Guidelines for the use of intrathecal and epidural diamorphine

Intrathecal and epidural opioid administration are well-established analgesic techniques in obstetrics. Diamorphine is a lipid soluble drug which, when given neuraxially provides good pain relief for up to 12 hours. This helps to extend the postoperative analgesia cover with less need for systemic opioid administration. Neuraxial route of administration produces a more targeted analgesic effect with less autonomic and systemic side effects compared to systemic routes. The use of diamorphine in both intrathecal and epidural routes is an established practice and is recommended by NICE1, OAA and AAGBI2.

Indications

  • LSCS

  • Trial of instrumental delivery in theatre

  • Major repair of perineal tears

Cautions

  • Intrathecal and epidural diamorphine should only be used in a theatre environment

  • Diamorphine should be freshly made for each patient.

  • Diamorphine should only be administered by consultant anaesthetists or by trainees once they have been assessed as competent

  • Intrathecal diamorphine must be prepared aseptically: i.e. the anaesthetist must be fully scrubbed (gloves, gown, hat and mask worn)3 and, by the anaesthetist over a sterile field immediately before the use of the drug and siting of the spinal injection.

  • Epidural diamorphine must be prepared as follows:

    • Clean an anaesthetic blue tray with an alcohol wipe.

    • Using sterile gloves and an aseptic non-touch technique reconstitute the diamorphine following the guidelines below.

    • Use an alcohol wipe to clean the epidural bung distal to the filter and disconnect. **Inject the diamorphine using an aseptic non-touch technique. Reconnect the bung.

Mode of reconstitution

  • Only 5mg diamorphine ampoules should be used to avoid confusion with other preparations of diamorphine.

  • The anaesthetist draws up 5mls sterile sodium chloride 0.9% into a sterile 5ml syringe via a 5μM filter needle3. The filter needle should be changed for a fresh filter needle before reconstituting the sodium chloride with the diamorphine. A filter needle should be used because diamorphine is stored in glass ampoules.

  • The neck of the diamorphine ampoule is wiped with an alcohol wipe by the anaesthetic assistant and allowed to dry prior to opening4.

  • The 5mg diamorphine powder is then reconstituted with the sterile sodium chloride 0.9% by the anaesthetist (giving a dilution of 1mg/ml).

  • Intrathecal diamorphine

    • The anaesthetist uses a sterile 1ml syringe to measure 0.3mls (300micrograms) diamorphine.

    • The remainder of the diamorphine (4.7mls) is immediately handed to the anaesthetic assistant to discard to avoid subsequent inadvertent use.

    • Do not retain on the spinal trolley

  • Epidural diamorphine

    • The dose of epidural diamorphine is 2.5 to 3mg. The anaesthetist discards 2 or 2.5ml from the 5ml diamorphine syringe leaving 2.5 or 3ml.

    • This can be injected epidurally. A further 2 to 3ml of sterile sodium chloride 0.9% should be drawn up and injected epidurally to flush the diamorphine that is left in the catheter and filter into the epidural space.

    • The timing for giving epidural diamorphine should be towards the end of the Caesarean Section procedure.

Management of potential problems following intrathecal/epidural diamorphine

Overdose of diamorphine is characterised by respiratory depression with or without CNS depression. The opioid antagonist naloxone is a specific antidote. As the duration of action of naloxone is considerably shorter than that of diamorphine then a continuous infusion of naloxone may be required (Clinical guideline CG10144-3).

There is the potential for delayed respiratory depression, which may be sudden and life threatening, occurring for up to 12 hours following intrathecal diamorphine administration.

The Post-Operative Intrathecal Diamorphine Protocol (2012a, b) provides instructions relating to the nursing and medical management of the following potential problems.

  • Inadequate pain relief

  • Depressed respiratory rate

  • Increased sedation

  • Motor block

  • Hypotension

  • Nausea and vomiting

  • Pruritus

  • Urinary retention

Guidelines for phenylephrine infusion during spinal anaesthesia

Exclude patients with pregnancy induced hypertension.

Trainees should not use phenylephrine infusion unless they have received instruction in its use.

Method

  • Dilute phenylephrine to 100mcg/ml

  • Add 10mg phenylephrine (1ml of 10mg/ml solution) to a 100ml bag of normal saline

  • Clearly label as phenylephrine 100mcg/ml, date, sign and add phenylephrine sticker

  • New bag made every morning or as required and left in Theatre 2 anaesthetic room.

  • Use a syringe driver with the facilities of delivering drugs in ml/hour and also having a bolus capability e.g. Grazeby 3400 anaesthesia pump.

  • Draw 20ml of the diluted phenylephrine into a 50ml luer lock syringe.

  • Prime a fine bore infusion line using the bolus facility on the syringe driver.

  • Connect the infusion line and the i.v. fluid line via a three-way tap connector, to an i.v. cannula. Use an anti-reflux valve to prevent the reflux of the vasopressor into the i.v. giving set.

  • Ensure that the i.v. fluid infusion runs continuously during the phenylephrine infusion to flush the dead space. Alternatively, use a dedicated i.v. cannula for the phenylephrine infusion.

Infusion protocol

  • Baseline maternal systolic blood pressure (SBP) should be measured before coming to theatre. NB. the SBP measured in the anaesthetic room immediately prior to spinal anaesthesia is often 10-20% above baseline.

  • Immediately following the spinal injection of local anaesthetic start the phenylephrine infusion at 20ml/ h (33mcg/min).

  • Measure SBP every minute initially

  • Measure heart rate continuously

  • Aim to keep SBP at baseline

  • Alter infusion rate by doubling or halving (range 2.5 - 40ml/h)

  • If SBP falls below baseline , double the set infusion rate (to maximum 40ml/h).

  • If SBP continues to decrease give 1ml boluses as required from the syringe driver.

  • Maternal hypotension (SBP < 75% of baseline) not responding to the maximum infusion rate of 40ml/h and 1ml boluses: give 3mg ephedrine and/or put the patient in the full left lateral position. Treat any associated bradycardia with glycopyrronium.

  • If the SBP increases above baseline , keep halving the infusion rate, and stop the infusion if < 2.5ml h-1 is required.

  • Maternal hypertension (SBP >125% of baseline): stop the infusion and restart if at half the rate once it has fallen below this threshold.

  • If heart rate is <60 bpm and the patient is hypotensive, or if the heart rate <45 bpm: give glycopyronnium 200mcg i.v.

Treating relative bradycardia (heart rate 50-60 bpm) with glycopyrronium when the SBP is normal, or increased, can lead to moderate / severe hypertension.

  • Discontinue infusion following delivery.

  • Ensure cannula is flushed.

Cell salvage in obstetrics

Although red cell salvage is used widely for operative procedures, there have been concerns about its use for obstetric patients. This is mainly due to the theoretical possibility of amniotic fluid embolus and possibility of increased risk of auto immunization of the Rhesus negative mother. By taking the precautions outlined, cell salvage may be safely used in obstetrics.

Recommendations

  • If practical, inform the mother about the use and benefits of the cell saver. An information leaflet is available.

  • It is recommended that only experienced operators use cell salvage in obstetrics. Trainees should inform the consultant on call if they need to use it.

  • It may be used for any situation in which allogeneic blood is used, but in practise this has so far been confined to caesarean sections, uterine re-exploration and laparotomy following postpartum haemorrhage.

  • The machine is set up and operated according to standard operating procedure, with an “in-continuity” set-up for Jehovah’s Witnesses should they request. (This means that the whole circuit is run through with saline and the retransfusion bag connected to the IV cannula before starting the salvage suction, thereby establishing a continuous circuit between the blood lost and the recipient vein.)

  • In cases where there is doubt about the extent of expected blood loss, it is economical to set up the aspiration and reservoir kit only.

  • The aim is to routinely set up the cell saver for cases of placenta praevia, postpartum haemorrhage, third or subsequent caesarean sections, Jehovah’s witnesses (if acceptable to the mother) and for mothers with pre-operative haemoglobin levels of 9 g/ dL or below. As staff expertise improves, the use of cell salvage will increase.

  • The decision to process and retransfuse can be made when the degree of haemorrhage has become clear.

  • Where practicable, amniotic fluid should be removed by separate suction prior to starting cell salvage. Collection of small amounts of amniotic fluid in the cell salvage reservoir is not a problem.

  • Suction should be via the wide bore sucker in the kit, and the surgeon should try to suction blood from “pools” rather than “dabbing” tissue surfaces with the sucker, which is known to disrupt erythrocytes.

  • Blood from swabs can be gently washed with saline and salvaged from a sterile bowl into the main reservoir.

  • To avoid red cell damage, suction pressure should be kept as low as practicable (<300mm Hg) although higher vacuum can probably be safely used if necessary.

  • It is mandatory to use the Pall RS Leucocyte Depletion filter in the retransfusion circuit if there is any risk of amniotic fluid contamination. This is currently the only filter that has been shown to remove all particulate elements of amniotic fluid (fetal squames, lamellar bodies). This will necessarily slow down the rate at which blood can be retransfused but a large volume syringe and 3-way tap could be used to speed up transfusion.

  • As with any transfusion, the patient should be carefully monitored for 24 hours. Coagulation tests should be sent post transfusion, and repeated if abnormal or if clinically indicated.

  • If the patient is Rhesus negative a Kleihauer test should be performed and Anti D given as appropriate within 72 hrs (the cell saver does not remove fetal red cells, which will be infused with the salvaged blood)

  • A national database of Obstetric Cell Salvage cases is currently being set up via the OAA. Details of this can be found with the consultant obstetric anaesthetists. Hospital units using cell salvage need to keep careful records for audit reporting in due course, with any problems also being reported to the Blood transfusion team as well as MHRA as per NICE Guidelines.

Pre–eclampsia and eclampsia

Maternal deaths from hypertensive disorders of pregnancy including pre-eclampsia and eclampsia are at the lowest rate since the start of the ‘Saving Mothers Lives’ reports in 1952. The current maternal mortality rate from the 2010-12 report is 0.38 per 100,000 maternities. The incidence of pre-eclampsia/eclampsia within the United Kingdom is 0.85 per 10,000 maternities. Fulminating pre-eclampsia can rapidly become life-threatening and needs to be managed using effective protocols.

Criteria for inclusion on the pre–eclampsia protocol

Woman with severe proteinuria and hypertension where the decision has been made to deliver and where one of the four following criteria are met below:

  1. Hypertension ≥140/90 with proteinuria of 2+ or more and at least one of the following:

    • Headache

    • Visual disturbances

    • Epigastric pain, pain just below ribs, liver tenderness or vomiting

    • Hyper-reflexia / sustained clonus (3 or more beats)

    • Platelets falling to less than 100 x 109/litre

    • Urate greater than 0.45 mmol/L

    • Abnormal liver enzymes (ALT or AST greater than 70 iu/L)

    • Oliguria, less than 500ml of urine in 24 hours

    • Serum Creatinine greater than 100umol/L

    • Proteinuria greater than 5g in 24hr

  2. Severe hypertension, systolic >150 mmHg or diastolic >110 mmHg, with proteinuria of 2+ or more

  3. Eclampsia

  4. Clinical discretion of the consultant obstetrician if there is atypical combination of symptoms

The Central Delivery Suite Coordinator is informed of any woman meeting the criteria for the pre-eclamptic / eclampsia protocol, who will then ensure the on-call Obstetric Registrar is informed, if not already aware.

The Obstetric Registrar informs the on-call Obstetric Consultant and the on call Anaesthetist of any woman who has commenced on the pre-eclamptic/eclampsia protocol.

The Central Delivery Suite Coordinator ensures the Neonatal Unit staff and paediatricians are informed.

Depending on the gestation of the baby the Consultant Obstetrician and Consultant Paediatrician will discuss the management of delivery and subsequent care of the baby.

Pre–eclampsia

The management of pre-eclampsia/eclampsia should follow the protocol irrespective of the mode of delivery and type of analgesia/anaesthesia used.

The designated pre-eclampsia/eclampsia protocol observation chart is commenced and observations are recorded meticulously.

The principles of management include:

  • Monitoring of vital signs

  • Haematological/biochemical evaluation

  • Strict fluid balance

  • Prophylactic use of Magnesium Sulphate to prevent fits

  • Control of hypertension

  • Antacid regime

  • Delivery of the baby

  • Thromboprophylaxis

Assessment and monitoring

Clinical examination

The cardiovascular, respiratory and central nervous systems are assessed by the obstetric registrar and / or on call anaesthetist on commencement of the protocol. The assessment must include state of the central nervous system, reflexes and the presence of clonus. All observations / findings are recorded on the pre-eclamptic observation chart and in the maternal health records.

Haematological/biochemical investigations

Pre-eclamptic blood screening should be carried out on commencement of the protocol and repeated 6 hourly prior to delivery and then 6 – 12 hours post-delivery.

  • Group and Save

  • Full blood count (FBC)

  • Urea and Electrolytes (U&E’s)

  • Liver Function Tests (LFT’s)

  • Uric Acid

  • Clotting studies (PT & APTT)

For regional analgesia or anaesthesia an FBC, and if appropriate coagulation screen is required within 4 hours prior to performing block.

Monitoring

The minimal observations required and recorded when a woman is cared for under the pre-eclamptic protocol regime are:

  • 15 minutes:

    • Maternal pulse

    • NIBP

    • MAP

  • Hourly:

    • Respiratory rate

    • Pulse oximetry (continuous monitoring but documented hourly)

    • Urine output

    • Fluid balance

    • Sedation level (continuous monitoring but documented hourly)

  • 6 hourly:

    • Temperature

    • Urine analysis

    • Reflexes

    • Bloods (pre-delivery): FBC, Coag, U&Es, LFTs, Urate

  • 6–12 hourly:

    • Bloods (post-delivery): FBC, Clotting, U&E’s, LFTs, Urate

    • Cardiotocography (CTG)

Fluid balance regime

  • Two 16G cannulae should be sited for adequate intravenous access.

  • Women on the protocol are to remain nil by mouth.

  • Whilst on the protocol Ranitidine 50mg intravenously should be given every 6 hours. This must be diluted with at least 20mls with Normal Saline and injected over at least 2 minutes.

  • Do not use volume expansion unless hydralazine is the anti-hypertensive used. If required Hartmanns 500mls over 60 minutes should be used before maintaining a strict hourly intake.

  • Hartmann’s solution is administered as maintenance fluid. The total fluid hourly intake is 80ml/hr.

  • The 80ml/hr takes into account all other intravenous infusions such as magnesium sulphate, syntocinon and anti-hypertensives plus drug boluses such as ranitidine but NOT fluid from an epidural infusion if sited.

  • Any blood loss should be replaced wiht an equal volume of blood or fluid. This fluid is not included in the 80ml/hr maintenance fluid regime. Document blood loss and replacement fluid on the fluid balance chart.

Urine output monitoring

Aim for a urine output of at least 25ml per hour or 100mls in 4 hours.

If the urine output is less than 25ml per hour over 4 consecutive hours the following action should be taken:

  • Discuss with the on-call Anaesthetist

  • The risk of pulmonary oedema is higher than that of renal failure in these women. However, if oliguria occurs, a bolus of 250ml colloid i.e. Gelofusin may be administered over 15 minutes if the woman shows no signs of pulmonary oedema.

  • To assess for pulmonary oedema the medical staff should ensure that the oxygen saturation is greater than 95% in air and the woman’s chest bases are clear on auscultation.

  • A further bolus of 250ml Gelofusin may be repeated once again after one hour. If oliguria persists, consider transferring to High Dependency Unit for central venous pressure monitoring.

Prophylactic use of magensium sulphate

Women commenced on the pre-eclampsia / eclamptic protocol should be given magnesium sulphate prophylactically using the regime:

  • Loading dose of 4g should be given intravenously over 5 minutes followed by and infusion of 1g/hour maintained for 24 hours

  • Recurrent seizures should be treated with 2-4g given over 5 minutes.

Magnesium therapy should not be given to women with myasthenia gravis, unless in critical care area.

If Nifedipine is used concurrently with Magnesium Sulphate, this may cause a more pronounced drop in the woman’s blood pressure. Ensure a vasopressor (ephedrine) is available.

Loading dose

  • 4g Magnesium Sulphate in 20mls (i.e. 20% solution) is given as a loading dose intravenously over 10 minutes by the Obstetric Medical Staff or Anaesthetist.

  • If using 50% Magnesium Sulphate take 8mls and dilute with 12mls 0.9% Sodium Chloride.

Maintenance infusion

  • 25g Magnesium Sulphate (50mls of 50% Magnesium Sulphate diluted to 250ml in Sodium Chloride 0.9% solution) is given at 10mls per hour. 50mls of Sodium Chloride will need to be removed and discarded from the 250ml bag first.

  • For 24 hours, an infusion of Magnesium Sulphate is continued at a rate of 1 g/hr per hour after delivery (or an eclamptic fit), which ever is later, unless there is a clinical reason to continue.

If convulsions occur, see management of Eclampsia

Side effects of magnesium

Initially there are transient side effects when administering a bolus of Magnesium Sulphate which can include:

  • Warmth/flushing

  • Nausea/vomiting

  • Slurred speech

  • Muscle weakness

  • Hypotension

  • Dizziness

  • Drowsiness

  • Headache

However, consider monitoring magnesium serum levels in women with prolonged oliguria over 12 hours.

Antihypertensive regime

Assess whether an antihypertensive regime is required. This will be based on systolic blood pressure, mean arterial blood pressure (MAP) readings on a blood pressure monitor. Treatment is aimed at achieving a systolic blood pressure of 150 mmHg and diastolic of between 80-100mmHg.

Labetalol is now considered to be the first line treatment for severe hypertension in pregnancy. (Magee et al 2003). Labetalol is contraindicated in women with 2nd or 3rd degree heart block and asthma. Hydralazine is no longer recommended as first line treatment as its side effects mimic symptoms of deteriorating pre-eclampsia.

sBP <150 mmHg and dBP 80–100 mmHg

No immediate treatment, but the blood pressure may still need treatment and must be regularly reviewed.

sBP >160 mmHg and dBP >100 mmHg

If blood pressure is greater than 160/100 then a bolus dose of Labetalol should be given before commencing the infusion.

10mg (2ml) of undiluted Labetalol is given intravenously over at least one minute and repeated at 5 minute intervals until the BP <155/105mmHg. This is administered by the Obstetric Medical Staff or Anaesthetist.

Once BP < 155/105 commence Labetalol infusion as indicated below.

Labetalol infusion

  • 40mls of neat Labetalol 5mg/ml.

  • Commence 20mg/hr (4ml per hour) and double infusion rate every 30 minutes until a systolic blood pressure is less than 150mmHg. Maximum dose 160mg/hr (32ml/per hour)

  • If the blood pressure is not controlled with 32mls per hour the on call Consultant Obstetrician should be informed.

Side effects of labetalol include postural hypotension, tiredness, weakness, and rashes.

If there is no response to one antihypertensive another should be tried. Some women are very resistant to treatment with Labetalol.

Hydralazine infusion

  • To be used if Labetalol is contraindicated

  • 40mg Hydralazine to 40ml with Sodium Chloride 0.9% solution (1mg /ml)

  • If rapid control required, 5mg Hydralazine intravenous diluted to 10mls with 0.9% Sodium Chloride injection as slow iv bolus over 3-4 minutes.

  • Repeat at 20-minute intervals to a cumulative dose of 20mg. Do not give more frequently.

  • Once the BP <155/105 commence infusion, starting at 5ml/hour

  • Increase every 30 minutes to max 18ml/hour

  • Do not increase infusion if heart rate is more than 120 per minute.

Side effects of hydralazine include tachycardia, hypotension, headache, and GI disturbance.

If the woman becomes hypotensive the infusion is stopped or reduced and the on call Anaesthetist called. The on call Anaesthetist will consider using a vasopressor such as ephedrine 3mg bolus.

Other antihypertensives

  • Oral Nifedipine 10mg, 6 hourly. Do not use slow release or sublingual.

  • The following drugs may only be used in a Critical Care area. Intra-arterial pressure monitoring is mandatory:

    • Glyceryl Trinitrate started at 10 microgram/kg/min and titrate to response. Monitor methaemoglobin levels.

    • Sodium Nitroprusside started at 0.25 microgram/kg/min and increase according to response.

Fetal assessment and delivery planning

  • Delivery should be planned with the intentions of having experienced staff available. (RCOG 2006)

  • Mode of delivery is decided according to parity, gestation and severity of maternal condition. A plan of action is formulated by the on-call Consultant, Paediatricians, and Anaesthetist and discussed with the parents.

  • In-utero transfer should not be encouraged as the safe management of the mother should be a priority.

  • If the woman has had an eclamptic fit then an urgent emergency caesarean section should be undertaken as soon as the woman’s condition is stable enough to allow an operation to be performed.

  • The woman’s general condition must be stabilised and adequate time needs to be given for control of the blood pressure prior to anaesthesia. (CEMACH 2003 - 05)

Analgesia

  • Epidural analgesia is the preferred method of pain relief in labour. A spinal, combined spinal-epidural or epidural top up is appropriate for operative delivery.

  • Before insertion of an epidural a recent (within the previous 4 hours) platelet count should be >100 x 109/L.

  • For platelet counts of 80–100 x 109/L a recent clotting screen (within the previous 4 hours) should be normal before regional anaesthesia.

  • Non-steroidal analgesics (diclofenac or ibuprofen) must not be prescribed for women with pre-eclampsia/eclampsia, as it impairs renal function and reduces platelet function.

Caesarean section – anaesthetic considerations

  • The on call Anaesthetist should inform the Consultant Anaesthetist.

  • Before anaesthesia the systolic blood pressure at or below 160mmHg.

  • The woman general condition must be stabilised and adequate time needs to be given for blood pressure control prior to anaesthesia. (CEMACH 2003 -05)

  • Encourage regional anaesthetic technique if not contraindicated. Both spinals and epidural have been used successfully. The usual technique would be a spinal using 2.5mls of 0.5% heavy bupivacaine with either 25mcg Fentanyl or 300mcg of Diamorphine.

  • Maintain blood pressure with small doses of vasopressors as required. Women with pre-eclampsia are less likely to become hypotensive compared to the normal pregnant woman. Therefore an anaesthetist should not routinely use a phenylephrine infusion.

  • Continue maintenance fluid intra-operatively.

  • Continue the Magnesium Sulphate infusion intraoperatively.

  • Any blood loss should be replaced with an equal volume of blood or fluid.

  • If general anaesthesia is necessary,the pressor response to laryngoscopy should be suppressed, most commonly with an opiate Alfentanil 10 – 20 micrograms/kg or Fentanyl. The paediatrician must be informed. Alternative drugs are:

    • Labetalol 10 – 15mg

    • Remifentanil 0.5mcg/kg/minute

    • Esmolol 100mg

  • Remember also to control hypertension at extubation.

  • Be prepared for a difficult oedematous airway and have small endotracheal tubes available.

  • Therapeutic levels of Magnesium Sulphate potentiate non-depolarising muscle relaxants. Monitor neuromuscular blockade.

  • Muscle fasciculations (involuntary muscle movements) may not occur with Suxamethonium in a woman treated with Magnesium Sulphate.

  • Syntocinon 5 units intravenously is administered following operative delivery. An infusion of 40 units Syntocinon with 36mls Saline Solution via a syringe pump at a rate of 10mls per hour is also administered post operatively.

Postoperative management

  • Transfer to a high dependency care may be indicated. (See Identification of the seriously ill woman and for those requiring High Dependency Care - Guideline WSH 2011)

  • If a general anaesthesia has been given, initial care will be provided in main theatre recovery prior to transfer to Central Delivery Suite.

  • Women with pre-eclampsia/ eclampsia who have been on the pre-eclamptic protocol should have a minimum of 24 hours post-delivery care on Central Delivery Suite.

  • Continue and maintain observations on designated MEOWS observation chart for pre- eclampsia.

  • Repeat full blood count, urea and electrolytes, and clotting screening 6 hours post- delivery then 6 – 12 hourly as clinically indicated.

  • Consult with the anaesthetist before removing an epidural catheter. The platelet count should be greater than 100,000/mm3 (or 80,000-99,999/mm3 with a normal clotting screen) prior to removing an epidural catheter if in situ. The anaesthetist must also ensure the timing of the last dose of Tinzaparin is appropriate.

  • Do not give non-steroidal analgesics (diclofenac or ibuprofen)

  • The care of the woman will be managed by Consultant Obstetrician and Consultant Anaesthetist. The woman’s clinical condition will dictate how frequently she is reviewed but this will be at least 6 hourly if not sooner.

  • At least 24 hours postpartum, or 24 hours after the last eclamptic fit the protocol can be discontinued. The decision is made following a review by a Consultant Obstetrician.

  • Women who have been on a pre-eclamptic / eclamptic protocol should be reviewed daily by the Obstetric Registrar.

  • 4 hourly blood pressure observations should be continued for 48 hours after caesarean section then 6 hourly for the remainder of the woman’s stay within hospital and recorded on the MEOWS chart.

  • Fluid balance charts must be maintained for a minimum of 24 hours after cessation of protocol.

On transfer to the postnatal ward a further 2 days stay post-delivery is advised to ensure observations are satisfactory prior to discharge home. (RCOG 2006)

If a eclamptic fit has occurred then she should stay a further 72 hours following discontinuation of the pre-eclamptic protocol.

Thromboprophylaxis

Anti-thromboembolic stockings should be worn until discharge home.

Tinzaparin subcutaneously will be required. This will be prescribed by the obstetrician in discussion with the anaesthetist according to the platelet count, coagulation studies and the woman’s weight.

Breastfeeding

  • >37 weeks gestation: normal breastfeeding should be encouraged if appropriate.

  • <37 weeks gestation: breastfeeding should be delayed for 24 hours after stopping magnesium and labetalol infusions. Encouragement should be given to express milk from the breasts in order to stimulate milk production. Any colostrum/milk obtained needs to be discarded.

Antihypertensives

If antihypertensive treatment is indicated after cessation of the pre-eclamptic protocol Labetalol 100mg twice daily should be commenced and increased as required, unless contraindicated.

If further treatment is required Nifedipine LA (20 – 30mg) is given as a starting dose. ACE inhibitors could be added following discussion with a Consultant Physician. Captopril or Enalapril would be ACE inhibitors of choice. (UKMI)

Blood pressure needs to be monitored, once a day, for a further two days when the woman is discharged home.

A Consultant Obstetrician outpatient appointment needs to be arranged for the six week postnatal examination.

Eclampsia

Eclampsia is defined as the occurrence of one or more convulsions (fits) in association with the syndrome of pre-eclampsia. (Cochrane 2009)

Note, 44% eclamptic fits occur in the postnatal period, 38% eclamptic fits occur before the onset of proteinuria or hypertension.

About 10% of women who have an eclamptic fit will have a further eclamptic fit after receiving a loading dose of magnesium sulphate. A further intravenous bolus will be required.

Principles of management follow the basic principles of Airway, Breathing, Circulation.

  1. Call for assistance by pressing the emergency call bell system, and ask for the assistance of the Central Delivery Suite Co-ordinator. The Central Delivery Suite Co- ordinator is actively involved in assisting/managing the woman who is having an eclamptic fit.

  2. The Central Delivery Suite Co-ordinator instructs that the Obstetric Registrar, and on call Anaesthetist are bleeped as an emergency call on 2222.

  3. The Obstetric Registrar informs the on-call Consultant Obstetrician.

  4. The Anaesthetist will request an Operating Department Practitioner (ODP)to attend to assist with anaesthesia if required.

Management of eclampsia

  1. Place the woman in the left lateral position and maintain the airway.

  2. Oxygen is administered via an oxygen mask with reservoir chamber at 15 L/min.

  3. Suction apparatus should be available to remove excessive oral secretions if the airways are becoming obstructed.

  4. Intravenous access with a 16G cannula,if not already present.

  5. The treatment for an eclamptic fit is magnesium sulphate.

Immediate control of eclampsia using magnesium sulphate

  • 4g of Magnesium Sulphate in 20mls (i.e. 20% solution) is given as a loading dose intravenously over 10 minutes by the Obstetric Medical Staff or Anaesthetist.

  • If using 50% Magnesium Sulphate take 8ml and dilute with 12ml 0.9% Sodium Chloride to a total of 20ml.

  • If further convulsions occur, a further bolus dose of 2- 4 g of Magnesium Sulphate is given over 10 minutes.

  • For repeated eclamptic fits, in spite of Magnesium Sulphate, Diazepam 10mg intravenously may be given. The on call anaesthetist must be contacted, bleep 770, if repeated fits continue. Consider other causes of convulsions if fits continue.

  • In the rare event of being unable to site an intravenous cannula, magnesium sulphate may be given intramuscularly. 4mls 50% (2 grams) is given by deep intramuscular injection into each buttock (total 4 grams)

See Prophylactic use of magensium sulphate for details on magnesium sulphate infusion regime, side effects and toxicity levels.

Magnesium sulphate infusion should be continued for 24 hours following delivery or 24 hours after the last eclamptic fit , whichever is the later, unless there is a clinical reason to continue.

HELLP syndrome

Corticosteroids such as Dexamethasone have been used in HELLP syndrome. The current evidence suggests they lead to a more rapid resolution of the biochemical and haematological abnormalities but there is no evidence that they reduce morbidity (Clenney 2004).

The use of Dexamethasone should be discussed with a Consultant Haemotologist prior to usage.

SIRS and sepsis during the antenatal, intrapartum, and postpartum period

Definitions

37.8 – 38.4ºC is common in the intrapartum period
Pyrexia

>38.5ºC may indicate an underlying infection
Antenatal period

Starts from confirmation of pregnancy up until the start of labour
Intrapartum period

Includes labour and delivery
Postnatal period

Starts immediately after delivery and lasts around 6 weeks
SIRS

Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome is an inflammatory state affecting the whole body, usually but not always due to an infection
Sepsis

Life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection.
Septic shock

Sepsis in which circulatory, cellular, and metabolic abnormalities are associated with a greater risk of mortality than with sepsis alone. These patients can be clinically identified by a vasopressor requirement to maintain a MAP ≥65 mmHg and serum lactate >2 mmol/L in the absence of hypovolaemia.

Severe sepsis and septic shock in pregnancy remain an important cause of maternal mortality in the UK. Nationally in 2003 - 2005 there were 13 deaths related to sepsis in pregnancy and substandard care was identified in many of these cases. Between 2006 - 2008 sepsis became the leading cause of maternal mortality, with deaths from Group A Streptococcus (GAS) accounting for 13 deaths.

In the most recent MBRRACE report 2009-2012 almost a quarter of women that died had sepsis, and 1 in 11 of the women died from flu.

Severe sepsis has a mortality rate of 20 - 40%, which increases to 60% if septic shock develops. Survival is often related to early recognition and prompt treatment, however previous CMACE reports have found that unfortunately recognition of sepsis is poor and guidelines on management are lacking.

Clinical care

The temperature of antenatal women is not recorded routinely. Pyrexia during pregnancy should be treated as clinical signs and symptoms indicate. If accompanied by symptoms such as abdominal pain, investigations should be instigated.

All healthcare professionals should be aware of the signs and symptoms of maternal sepsis and of the rapid, potentially lethal course of septic shock. These signs may be less obvious in a pregnant patient compared to non-pregnant patients so a high index of suspicion is needed.

Clinical signs include: * Pyrexia/hypothermia * Tachycardia/hypotension * Tachypnoea/hypoxia * Oligouria * Impaired consciousness * Failure to respond to treatment

Regular, frequent observations of all vital signs should be recorded on a MEOWs chart. This should include temperature, pulse rate, blood pressure and respiratory rate.

Maternal temperature should be checked and recorded at least 4 hourly during labour. Low grade pyrexia (37.8 – 38.4ºC) is common in labouring women, it is a strenuous process, the rooms are kept warm for delivery and a working epidural may alter the patients temperature.

A pyrexia of >38.5ºC may indicate underlying infection and needs to be investigated and treated as appropriate. High grade pyrexia in labour >38.5ºC (single reading) or clinical suspicion of infection is a serious complication requiring urgent attention; it may be in indicator of dehydration or infection, including Group B Streptococcus (GBS) infection which can lead to chorioamnionitis, sepsis and increased maternal and neonatal morbidity. The Intrapartum Pyrexia (>38.5ºC) care pathway should be followed.

If no improvement with treatment or if the patient deteriorates despite starting antibiotics then inform the obstetric and anaesthetic teams and transfer to consultant led care if not already, and commence continuous EFM.

All investigations should be taken prior to commencement of antibiotics – however, do not delay antibiotic administration, they should be given within 1 hour of diagnosis of suspected sepsis. Follow the Empirical Treatment of Suspected for choice of antibiotics but further advice is available from the consultant microbiologist on call. Commence IV fluids and a start fluid balance chart. A Paediatrician should be present for delivery

If SIRS or sepsis is suspected in a pregnant woman of any gestation the Obstetric Sepsis Screening Tool should be used. This has been modified from commonly used adult sepsis screening tools to take into consideration the altered physiology of pregnancy, most of which will have occurred by the end of the first trimester.

After delivery – investigations and observation:

  • Take swabs from baby’s ear, throat and umbilicus, as well as amnion and chorion swabs from the placenta

  • In case of suspected severe infection send placenta to histology

  • Baby monitored unless antibiotics required in which case admit to NNU

  • 4 hourly maternal observations until normal for 2 readings (complete MEOWS chart)

  • If prolonged rupture of membranes and other signs of infection give full course of antibiotics (discuss with microbiology)

  • Amend treatment when results of investigations (i.e. culture and sensitivities) are available – do not discharge until results known

  • If still pyrexial or has other symptoms after 24 hours of IV antibiotics then discuss with on call consultant microbiologist

Sepsis in the antenatal or intrapartum period

Risk factors for maternal sepsis (CMACE 2011)

  • Obesity

  • Anaemia

  • Vaginal discharge

  • History of pelvic infection

  • Of black/minority ethnic group origin

  • Cervical cerclage

  • Impaired glucose tolerance/diabetes

  • Impaired immunity/immunosuppressant medications

  • Amniocentesis and other invasive procedures

  • History of group B streptococcal infection

  • Prolonged spontaneous rupture of membranes

  • GAS infection in close contacts/family members

Signs and symptoms of SIRS and sepsis

A useful reminder of the signs and symptoms to look for which indicate SIRS is 3T’s (white with sugar):

  • Temperature <36ºC or >38.5ºC

  • Tachycardia >100bpm (pre-delivery)

  • Tachypnoea >20/min

  • White cell count <4 x 109/L or >12 x 109/L

  • Glucose >7.7mmol/L (in the absence of diabetes)

Signs and symptoms suggestive of infection and which require further investigation:

  • Fevers or rigors

  • Diarrhoea and/or vomiting – may indicate endotoxin production and toxic shock syndrome may develop

  • Rash (generalised maculopapular rash or purpura fulminans)

  • Abdominal or pelvic pain – which does not respond to usual analgesia

  • Offensive or significant vaginal discharge

  • Productive cough / breathlessness

  • Urinary symptoms – renal angle tenderness, dysuria and frequency

  • An absent fetal heart with or without placental abruption may be as a result of sepsis

Indications of organ dysfunction:

  • Bilateral lung infiltrates and new need for oxygen to maintain sats >95%

  • Lactate >2mmol/L

  • Creatinine >176.8 μmol/L or UO < 0.5ml/kg/hr for 2 hours

  • Platelets < 100 x 109/L, INR >1.5, APTT > 60s

  • Bilirubin >70μmol/L

  • Acute confusion

Management of suspected sepsis

Blood cultures are the key investigation and should ideally be obtained prior to giving antibiotics along with other samples as guided by clinical suspicion of the focus of infection. Samples that should be considered for collection depending on symptoms include:

  • Urine

  • HVS

  • Placenta swabs

  • Wound swabs (e.g. LSCS)

  • Throat swab (bacterial)

  • Sputum

  • Stool

  • CSF

  • Nose and throat swab (viral transport medium available on request from lab)

  • Red-topped swabs for MRSA screen (nose AND groin) should also be sent

Broad spectrum antibiotic treatment should be started within 1 hour and should not be delayed by the collection of these samples – each hour delay in antibiotic treatment is associated with a measurable increase in mortality. Give appropriate antibiotic treatment by reviewing patients previous microbiology results if any are available, if postnatal review the baby’s microbiology results as well. Advice and support is always available from the on call consultant microbiologist. Antibiotic effectiveness should be assessed daily and monitor toxicity levels as per hospital guidelines (gentamicin and vancomycin). Change to narrow spectrum when results from cultures are available.

CXR may be indicated if there are signs and symptoms of respiratory distress. Prompt imaging may also help localise the source of infection (and therefore treatment) and should not be deferred on the grounds of pregnancy.

Serum lactate should be measured within six hours of suspicion of severe sepsis in order to guide management. Lactate >2mmol/L is indicative of tissue hypoperfusion and requires fluid resuscitation. A lactate of >4 mmol/L suggests septic shock and needs input by ITU. Arterial blood gases may help assess hypoxia.

Administration of a crystalloid fluid challenge to restore circulating volume and mean arterial pressure is important. If there is no response to this sepsis may be severe and fluid management may be managed better with input from critical care outreach/ITU.

Toxic shock syndrome

Toxic shock syndrome is caused by staphylococcal or streptococcal exotoxins and can cause confusing symptoms including:

  • Nausea, vomiting and diarrhea

  • Severe pain out of proportion to clinical signs due to necrotising fasciitis

  • Watery vaginal discharge

  • Generalised rash

  • Conjunctival suffusion

Deterioration may be rapid and there is a high mortality associated with the syndrome.

Further management

In severe or rapidly deteriorating cases the senior midwife and consultant obstetrician on call should be informed and involved in care. An urgent referral to critical care should also be made for assessment. Advice regarding antimicrobials may be required from the microbiologist on call.

A MEOWs chart should be used to help identify seriously ill or deteriorating patients.

Help and advice from critical care is available via the critical care outreach nurses or from the on call ITU doctor via bleep 759.

The decision to transfer to intensive care should be made by the critical care team in conjunction with the obstetric consultant on call and the obstetric anaesthetist on call.

Monitoring and delivery of fetus

Severe sepsis is associated with preterm labour. Delivery may be considered if the woman is critically ill as this may be beneficial to the mother, baby or both. Attempting delivery in the setting of maternal instability increases fetal and maternal mortality rates unless the source of infection is intrauterine. A decision on mode and timing of delivery should be made by the consultant obstetrician on call. If preterm delivery is anticipated cautious use of steroids may be appropriate to aid foetal lung maturity – however, steroids are an immunosuppressant and so consideration of maternal well-being is important.

Continuous electronic fetal monitoring is recommended in the intrapartum period in women with pyrexia or proven sepsis. Changes in the CTG such as changes in baseline, variability or new onset decelerations should prompt reassessment of maternal MAP, hypoxia and acidaemia as these changes may be due to worsening maternal condition.

Sepsis in the postalnatal period

If sepsis is suspected in the immediate 24-48 hours post-delivery then use the Obstetric Sepsis Screening Tool. The onset of sepsis can be insidious however and may not reveal itself for several days postpartum when most women will be at home. Where infection is suspected >48hours up to six weeks post-partum, also use the Obstetric Sepsis Screening Tool.

  • Full history should be taken

  • Urgent referral should be made to the on call specialist registrar

  • Observations should be undertaken and documented on a MEOWS chart, including use of The Obstetric Sepsis Screening Tool and Obstetric Sepsis Care Bundle

  • Broad spectrum antibiotics should be started

  • Continued assessment should be made and antibiotics rationalised in view of any previous or current microbiology results

  • Early discussion with consultant microbiologist if needed

Modified early warning scores (MEOWS)

Early warning scoring has proved effective in the general hospital population for the recognition of the unwell or deteriorating patient and prevention of cardiac arrest. Development of scoring systems in the obstetric population has proved more difficult, due to altered physiological parameters of the parturient. However, the recent CMACE reports have highlighted the dangers of not being alert to the deterioration of a sick pregnant or recently delivered woman and recommended the use of an early warning system.

The 2007 NICE Clinical Guideline ‘Acutely ill Patients in Hospital’ states that all adult patients in an acute hospital setting should have their physiological observations recorded and a scoring system should be used for detecting deteriorating health.

Pain is now the ‘5th vital sign’ which the Trust has signed up to. Trust wide audits of pain scoring will be performed, which will include Womens and Childrens Directorate.

MEOWS charts

The West Suffolk MEOWS charts are broadly based on the MEWS (Modified Early Warning Score) charts used on every patient in West Suffolk Hospital with changes to reflect the physiology of pregnancy.

The chart has been developed for maternal safety in response to national recommendations.

MEOWS chart protocol

  • Charts will be used for all women admitted to F11 and CDS.

  • Charts should also be started in AAU or in the Accident and Emergency Department

  • There is plenty of space on the charts, so new charts need not be started for each admission.

  • It is important to realise that there is no increased frequency of observations (unless the woman deteriorates)

  • However, EVERY observation is scored (see exceptions below) by any member of staff currently able to perform observations

  • The minimum physiological observations to be recorded and scored are:

    • Respiratory rate

    • Pulse

    • Systolic Blood Pressure

    • Diastolic Blood Pressure

    • Urine output (if hourly measurements are in progress) * Temperature

    • Neurological status

  • Each of the above parameters are scored as per scoring schedule shown on the chart

  • If a threshold score of 3 (without hourly urine measurement) or 4 (with hourly urine measurement) is triggered, the call out cascade must be instituted as shown by the flow diagram on the front of the chart

  • Document the action undertaken if there is a trigger score (a table is on the chart)

  • With every observation, pain scoring must be undertaken. This will be subject to Trust wide scrutiny as one of the main corporate targets driven by the Dept of Health.

  • A treatment algorithm is available on CDS and F11 to ensure appropriate analgesia is given to women

  • There is space to record PCA and epidural pump observations

  • All epidural observations are now recorded on the MEOWS chart (see below for further details) rather than on the reverse of the pink epidural chart

  • The midwifery postnatal wardex observation chart is on the back page of the MEOWS chart for ease of photocopying for transfer use to the community

  • The observation frequency following operative deliveries has been modified. Full details are on the chart

Labour

The use of the chart in labour is doubtlessly the most difficult area to address when the mother has the modifying influences of pain and anxiety on her observations. However, even with the most straightforward labour there can be unheralded catastrophes. It would therefore be unwise to deny the women the same level of vigilance as those who are not in labour.

MEOWS observations if no epidural insitu

  • All women have a full MEOWS score on admission to CDS

  • Record 4 hourly observations as a minimumon the MEOWS chart with scores

  • Only increase the observations if the woman has triggered the call out cascade

  • All women have full MEOWS score within 30 minutes of delivery

  • There is no requirement to undertake pain scoring for women in the latent phase of labour or in active labour

Women admitted to CDS with pain unassociated with labour do require recording of pain scores.
MEOWS observations if epidural insitu

  • All women have a full MEOWS score on admission to CDS

  • All women have a full MEOWS score prior to epidural siting

  • There after hourly MEOWS score (use temperature from previous observation if not due)

  • Continue with the 5 minute observations of blood pressure and pulse after the initial bolus or any top up for 30 minutes. No MEOWS score unless the observations have markedly changed during this period

  • Pain scores must be performed hourly

  • All women have full MEOWS score within 30 minutes of delivery

Epidural monitoring

  • Hourly sensory block heights during labour (dermatome diagrams are available).

  • Bilateral T10 (umbilical level) is an appropriate height for labour

  • It is Trust-wide policy that all patients who have had epidurals are followed up to allow early identification of neurological symptoms due to any developing epidural haematoma. 4 hourly motor block must be recorded for 24 hours after the catheter has been removed (scored by midwife on the MEOWS chart).

  • Motor block scoring also needs to be done if there has been an attempt at an epidural as the mother may be subject to similar side effects as those who have actually had one inserted

  • Spinals do not need postoperative motor block observations (they pose a much lower risk of haematoma)

  • There is an immediate recovery record for use in Room J or in the main theatre recovery

  • The anaesthetist must fill in the type of anaesthetic and the dose of spinal / epidural opioid

  • There are facilities to record maternal observations, both physiological and obstetric (recovery and CDS), epidural observations and PCA data (usually recovery only)

  • Frequency of observations are colour highlighted on the chart

  • Subsequent observations are then recorded on the MEOWS chart

  • The checklist must be completed and signed by a trained midwife / nurse, prior to ward transfer
